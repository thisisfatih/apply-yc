# Critique Rubric

When the founder hands over an existing draft, walk through it with the partner mindset. The goal is honest, specific feedback that improves the application - not soft validation.

## The partner mindset

Imagine you've already read 50 applications today and have 50 more to go. You give each one ~2-4 minutes initially. You're looking for reasons to put it in the "yes, interview" pile, but the default is "no" - there are too many applications and too few slots.

What makes you put one in the "yes" pile in 2 minutes:
- Sentence 1 of "what is your company going to make" gives you a concrete picture in your head
- The "impressive thing" answer contains a specific, verifiable, hard achievement
- The traction numbers, however small, are real and specific
- The competitor analysis names real competitors and explains the moat
- You can imagine a partner enjoying a 10-minute conversation with these founders

What makes you reject in 30 seconds:
- The first sentence is buzzword soup
- The "impressive thing" is a generic adjective ("dedicated", "passionate", "world-class")
- Numbers are missing or vague ("growing fast", "lots of users")
- "No competitors" or "we have no real competition"
- The team has no technical co-founder and no plan for one
- The video is over a minute, scripted, or missing

## Walk through the draft in this order

The order matters because the partner reads it in this order, and a weak top kills everything below.

### 1. The 50-character pitch (A4)
**Test:** Read it to someone outside your industry. If they can't paraphrase what your company does in 5 seconds, it fails.
**Common failures:**
- Buzzwords ("AI-powered platform for enterprise teams")
- Too abstract ("operating system for X")
- Too narrow ("React component library for forms")
- Cute but unclear ("the Stripe of dirt")

**Rewrite pattern:** Try the X-but-Y formula. Try a concrete user-action statement. Iterate 5-10 versions, pick the best.

### 2. "What is your company going to make?" (A5)
**Test:** After reading the answer, can the reader reproduce the idea in their own head?
**Common failures:**
- Mission/vision language at the top ("We're transforming the way teams collaborate")
- Tech-first description instead of user-first ("We've built a multi-tenant LLM orchestration layer")
- Burying the actual product 4 sentences in
- Using the company's own jargon instead of the industry's standard terms

**Rewrite pattern:** First sentence is a TL;DR - what the user does with it. Second sentence is what makes it different. Third sentence (if needed) is where it goes long-term. Cut everything else.

### 3. Founder section - "impressive thing" (A8, B2)
**Test:** Is each thing listed (a) specific, (b) hard, (c) verifiable?
**Common failures:**
- Generic adjectives ("exceptionally driven", "world-class engineer")
- The startup itself listed as the impressive thing (waste of opportunity)
- Things that sound impressive but aren't ("led a team of 50" without saying what they shipped)
- Resume-shaped answers (titles and companies, not actual achievements)

**Rewrite pattern:** Replace each adjective with a fact. "Drove growth at X" → "Took X from $1M to $10M ARR in 18 months as employee #4." "Strong technical background" → "Built and shipped Y, used by Z thousand people."

### 4. Founder section - "interesting project together" (B3)
**Test:** Does this prove the cofounders ship together?
**Common failures:**
- "We met in college" with no project named
- A very small thing (a hackathon weekend) presented as bigger than it was
- No URL/evidence

**Rewrite pattern:** If there's no real shared project, don't fake one. Add a sentence acknowledging the gap and what you've shipped together so far ("We met in March; since then we've built and launched the MVP and gotten our first 12 paying customers"). Honesty > a stretched answer.

### 5. Progress section (C2-C6)
**Test:** Are the numbers specific and is the trajectory clear?
**Common failures:**
- "Lots of interest", "early traction", "growing fast" - vague
- Conflating signups with active users
- Listing GMV instead of revenue
- Hiding the small numbers ("we have early users" instead of "12 paying customers, $400 MRR")

**Rewrite pattern:** Replace every adjective with a number or remove it. If the numbers are small, state them as-is - Basedash got in with $30/month.

### 6. "Why this idea / domain expertise" (D1)
**Test:** Did the founder pick this idea for a defensible reason?
**Common failures:**
- "We saw a $50B market opportunity" - pure pitch-speak, no real origin
- "Our team has decades of experience in X" - generic
- No personal connection to the problem

**Rewrite pattern:** Find the real origin story. Did you have this problem yourself? Did you build it for a previous job? Did you talk to N users? State that origin in concrete terms.

### 7. "What's new / substitutes" (D2)
**Test:** Does this make the alternatives sound painful?
**Common failures:**
- "There's nothing like this" - almost never true, signals lack of research
- Vague alternatives ("Excel, basically") - name actual products
- Listing competitors here instead of explaining what users do today

**Rewrite pattern:** State the real workarounds users employ today, in specific painful terms. Then explain what your approach does differently - at the abstraction level, not the feature level.

### 8. "Competitors / who do you fear" (D3)
**Test:** Are the competitors real, named, and is the moat believable?
**Common failures:**
- "We have no competitors" - instant rejection signal
- Naming only one or two and missing obvious ones
- Hand-waving on the moat ("we have a better UX")
- Not naming the one they actually fear

**Rewrite pattern:** List 3-5. Pick the most threatening. State why you can win or coexist with each. Look at Dropbox's S07 answer for the gold standard - 6 competitors, specific reason for each.

### 9. "How will you make money" (D5)
**Test:** Is the business model named and is the upper bound estimated bottoms-up?
**Common failures:**
- "Subscription" without a price
- "Massive market" without a calculation
- Implausibly large numbers ($100B TAM for a niche product)

**Rewrite pattern:** Name the model, name the price, do a rough bottoms-up: "There are X customers, we can charge $Y/year, that's $Z." Pick a comparable public company for upper bound if useful.

### 10. The wildcard hack question (A7)
**Test:** Is it specific, slightly cheeky, and not the startup itself?
**Common failures:**
- The startup as the answer ("we hacked the market by…")
- Something obviously legal/conventional ("I got into Harvard")
- Vague claims ("I'm a hustler")

**Rewrite pattern:** Find a real instance of the founder beating a system in their personal history. Doesn't need to be glamorous - getting into an event for free, talking your way into a class, negotiating a free year of office space, a creative visa hack.

### 11. The video
See `video-and-demo.md`. Most common failures: too long, too produced, missing co-founders, bad audio.

### 12. The amusing-thing question (F2)
**Test:** Does it feel human and not contrived?
**Common failures:**
- Trying too hard to be quirky
- Humble-brag dressed as amusement
- Skipping it (it's there for a reason)

**Rewrite pattern:** Something genuinely small and personal. Both Dropbox's and Basedash's answers are 1 sentence each.

## Reapplication check (do this first, before question-by-question)

Before going question-by-question, scan the full draft for reapplication signals. See `reapplying.md`, section "Detecting implicit reapplication signals".

If this is a reapplication (explicit or implicit):
- The "what changed since last time" field becomes the most important question on the application. If it's empty or says N/A, flag it before anything else.
- Every progress number should be read as a delta: current state vs. state at last application.
- If the "amusing discovery" or any other field references a prior YC interview, note it; the founder may not realize they've signaled it.

## Final pass

After question-by-question critique, do these holistic checks:

**The "red pen" pass.** Print mentally. Cross out every word that doesn't pull weight. Specifically watch for: leverage, synergy, next-generation, revolutionize, transform, AI-powered (without concrete content), platform (when "tool" or "product" would do), seamless, world-class, best-in-class, cutting-edge.

**The "stand alone" check.** Pick any 3 questions at random. Read just those answers. Do they make sense without the others? They should - partners may not read in order.

**The "TL;DR" check.** For every answer over 1 sentence, does the first sentence summarize the whole answer? It should.

**The honesty check.** Are there any answers that overstate, hide a problem, or use vague language to avoid stating something inconvenient? Flag them. Partners can tell, and it ages badly through the interview.

**The momentum check.** Is there evidence of progress between when the founder started and now? If everything is "we plan to" or "we will", that's a problem. Find the recent shipped thing.
