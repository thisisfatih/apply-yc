# Founder Video & Demo Guidelines

The founder video is the highest-leverage part of the application after the pitch and "what is your company going to make" answer. **Applications with videos are statistically much more likely to get an interview** (Paul Graham confirmed this directly).

## The video

### The hard rules
- **1 minute. Maximum.** Not 1:30. Not 2 minutes. Many applications get rejected for going over.
- **Unlisted YouTube link.** Not "private" (YC can't view). Not Vimeo or Loom (their guidelines specify YouTube).
- **All founders on camera.** If you have co-founders, every co-founder appears in the video. This is non-negotiable.
- **Introduce yourselves.** Name, role, and what your company does.

### What YC wants to see
- The people behind the application. They're trying to figure out whether they want to work with you for 3 months and then for years.
- Co-founder dynamic. If you have co-founders, the video is the best evidence of how you interact.
- That you can communicate clearly without a polished script.

### What kills videos
- **Background music.** YC explicitly dislikes this. It signals overproduction.
- **Reading from a script.** Reads as wooden. Bullet points to remember are fine; reading sentences is bad.
- **Cinematic edits, transitions, b-roll.** This is not a launch video. It's a 60-second introduction.
- **Stock footage or animation.** Same issue.
- **Buzzword soup.** "We're revolutionizing the way…" - exact same problem as in the written application.
- **Bad audio.** Visual quality matters less than audio. Use a decent mic or even your phone in a quiet room. Don't use a laptop mic in an echoey space.
- **Missing a co-founder.** "Bob couldn't make it today" is a yellow flag at minimum.
- **Going over 1 minute.** This signals you can't follow simple instructions.

### A tested structure (60 seconds)
- 0:00-0:10 - "Hi, I'm [name], [role at company]. I'm [name], [role]. We're [company]." (Each founder speaks.)
- 0:10-0:30 - What the company does, in plain English. Same as the 50-character pitch but expanded slightly.
- 0:30-0:45 - Who you are and why you're the right team for this. One sentence per founder on the most relevant background.
- 0:45-0:60 - Where you are right now (launched, building, X users) and what you'll do with YC.

This is a structure, not a script. Memorize the points; speak naturally.

### Setup checklist
- Quiet room. No echo. No air conditioning humming.
- Decent lighting. Window in front of you, not behind. No backlit silhouettes.
- Decent mic. AirPods or a $30 USB mic beats the laptop mic.
- Phone camera at eye level (not below - no chin-cam) or laptop camera with the laptop raised on books.
- All co-founders in frame, not split-screen unless you literally cannot be in the same room.
- Test record 30 seconds first. Listen back. Adjust.

### Common failure modes Claude should flag

If the founder is asking for help with the video and shows signs of overproduction (mentioning script, planning B-roll, considering music, planning to "do another take with better lighting and edits"): tell them to stop. The over-produced video is a worse outcome than a slightly rough one. YC has explicitly said this.

If the founder asks Claude to write a video script: don't. Offer to draft a structure or talking points instead, but the script itself defeats the purpose. Suggest the founder do 3-4 unscripted takes and pick the best.

## The demo

### What "demo" means
A working version of whatever the product is. Not a mockup. Not a Figma file. Not a slide. A thing the partner can either click on or watch you click on.

### Acceptable formats
- **Live link** - best, if the product is online and works without a complicated setup
- **Screen recording (Loom, QuickTime)** - second best, especially if the product needs login, specific data, or a guided tour
- **Demo video** - for hardware or for products that need to be shown in physical context
- **Login credentials** - if behind auth, include working credentials in the demo URL field

### What good demos look like
- Show the actual user flow. Not the marketing site. Not a landing page. The thing users do.
- 30-90 seconds is enough. Don't ramble.
- Show data, not empty states. A real-looking task, lead, customer, document - not "Lorem Ipsum".
- Show the magic moment first if possible. The thing that makes someone go "oh, that's clever."

### What kills demos
- A landing page tour ("here's our hero section, here's our features section…"). Nobody cares.
- Empty state demos with no data
- Mocks instead of working software
- Long preambles ("So, the problem we're solving is…") - they've read your application; just show it
- A demo of a feature you haven't built yet without saying so

### What to do if the product isn't ready
Be honest. A 30-second screen recording of a working prototype with rough edges beats a polished mockup of a thing that doesn't exist. Drew Houston (Dropbox) submitted a screencast of a working private beta - that's the bar.

If you literally have nothing to demo, focus the rest of the application harder. The "where do you live / what's your impressive thing / who's the team" answers carry more weight when there's no demo.
