# YC Application Questions - Full Reference

Current as of Summer 2026. The form is a single-page application at `apply.ycombinator.com`. Founder profile details (accomplishments, background) are filled on per-founder sub-pages, then the main form is completed.

---

## Founder Profile (per founder - fill first)

Each founder has a profile sub-page with five sections: Basics, Role & Responsibilities, Background, Social Media, Accomplishments. The Accomplishments section contains the highest-stakes per-founder questions.

### Basics
Name, email, age (dropdown), phone (with country code), gender, city.

### Role & Responsibilities
- Title / main responsibility (CEO, CTO, etc.)
- Equity percent (number field)
- **Are you a technical founder?** Yes/No. YC's definition: you can build the product without outside assistance. This is a primary filter. Michael Seibel: "no technical co-founder" is one of the top-3 application killers.
- Currently in school: Yes/No
- **Exclusive commitment:** "If accepted to YC, will you commit to working exclusively on this project for the next year?" Required yes. If you're a student, this means deferring or dropping out.

### Background
- LinkedIn URL (required)
- Education (structured entries: school, dates, degree)
- Work History (structured entries: company, role, dates, description) - YC may ask for references if accepted

### Social Media
- Personal website
- GitHub URL
- Twitter URL

### Accomplishments - HIGH STAKES

#### "Please tell us about a time you most successfully hacked some (non-computer) system to your advantage." ⭐ THE WILDCARD
Paul Graham explicitly says this question is a wildcard that can rescue an otherwise weak application. YC is looking for people who beat systems.

**Rubric:**
- Specific and concrete: names, what you did, what you got
- Doesn't have to be glamorous. Getting a visa via obscure category, negotiating free office space, convincing a university to give credit for a startup - all work
- Should show resourcefulness, hustle, creative problem-solving outside software
- Don't repeat your startup. That's not the spirit.

**Real examples that worked:**
- Basedash S20: "I managed to continue working on my company as my capstone project for school until I graduate. I also recruited a technical student as a partner working full-time hours for free (don't worry, they signed an NDA & IP agreement)."
- Convincing a school to count startup as course credit; getting a visa via obscure path; cold-emailing into a job that required 10 years experience; reverse-engineering a system for legitimate advantage.

#### "Please tell us in one or two sentences about the most impressive thing other than this startup that you have built or achieved." ⭐⭐ HIGH STAKES
Paul Graham: **"To me this is the most important question on the application."**

**Rubric:**
- It's not the type of achievement that matters - it's the magnitude
- DON'T list the current startup. They already know.
- Pick the thing that was hardest. Specific facts beat adjectives. Numbers, names, stakes.
- One or two sentences only.

**Dropbox S07:**
> Drew - Programming since age 5; startups since age 14; 1600 on SAT; started profitable online SAT prep company in college (accoladeprep.com). For fun last summer reverse engineered the software on a number of poker sites and wrote a real-money playing poker bot (it was about break-even).

5 specific things in 2 sentences. Each one verifiable and hard.

**Bad answer:**
> Jordan is an exceptionally dedicated person who gives 100% effort to every project he undertakes.

(Paul Graham uses this verbatim as the example of what NOT to write.)

#### "Tell us about things you've built before."
Apps, sites, open source contributions. Include URLs. This is where a portfolio of shipped work compounds. Don't just list names - include enough context to understand what you built and at what scale.

#### "List any competitions/awards you have won, or papers you've published."
Patents, grants, press, academic papers, competition wins. Include dates and relevance. A European patent or Google Cloud grant reads differently than a hackathon ribbon.

## Main Application

All on one page at `apply.ycombinator.com/apps/[id]/edit`. Sections in order:

---

## Founders (main form)

### "Who writes code, or does other technical work on your product? Was any of it done by a non-founder? Please explain."
The technical-founder check. Best answer: a founder wrote everything. If contractors built any of the core product, address it directly and explain the plan to bring tech in-house.

### "Are you looking for a cofounder?"
Fill if actively seeking. Leave blank or answer "no" if team is complete.

### Co-founder questions (appear when second founder is added)
When you add a co-founder, additional questions appear:
- Interesting project two or more of you created together (include URLs - if nothing, that's a gap)
- How long have the founders known one another and how did you meet?
- Have any of the founders not met in person?

---

## Founder Video

File upload. 1 minute, all founders on camera, no script, decent audio. Max 100MB.
See `video-and-demo.md` for full guidance. Applications with videos get interviews at significantly higher rates.

## Company

### Company name
Just the name. Founders rename later. Don't agonize.

### "Describe what your company does in 50 characters or less." ⭐ HIGH STAKES
Partners use this to decide whether to keep reading. Goal: reader has a clear picture in their head after 4 seconds.

**Rubric:**
- Concrete, not abstract ("Collaborative database interface for teams" yes / "AI platform for enterprises" no)
- No buzzwords: "revolutionizing", "AI-native", "platform", "next-gen" are all automatic downgrades
- X-but-Y is the gold standard if X is universally recognized (Uber, Airbnb, eBay, Slack, Excel work; Wolfram, Datadog, Stripe-for-non-tech-people don't)
- Read it to someone outside your industry. If they don't get it, rewrite.

**Good examples:**
- "Collaborative database interface for teams." (Basedash S20)
- "AI that writes SEC filings in 10 minutes."
- "Stripe for B2B invoices in Latin America."

**Bad examples:**
- "AI-powered platform for enterprises" (means nothing)
- "Revolutionizing the $3tn trade finance market" (marketing-speak, no content)

### Company URL
Live site. If none, leave blank. A broken or placeholder Squarespace is worse than nothing.

### Demo
File upload or video link. Anything showing the product working. Max 3 min / 100MB. If login required, provide credentials. **Applications with a demo get interviews at much higher rates.** A 90-second Loom beats nothing.

### Product link + login credentials
Direct link to the working product with any login needed to access it.

### "What is your company going to make? Please describe your product and what it does or will do." ⭐⭐ HIGHEST STAKES
The most important question on the application. First question of the interview. The partner reads this with the 50-char pitch in mind.

**Rubric:**
- 2-4 sentences. First sentence is TL;DR.
- Matter-of-fact, not promotional. Test: after reading, can the partner reproduce the idea in their head?
- X-but-Y if it fits. Don't be afraid of seeming derivative - partners love clarity over originality in pitch language.
- Describe what users do with it, not what the tech is.
- Cover current state (1-2 sentences) and future direction (1 sentence). Skip vision/mission language.

**Dropbox S07:**
> Dropbox synchronizes files across your/your team's computers. It's much better than uploading or email, because it's automatic, integrated into Windows, and fits into the way you already work. There's also a web interface, and the files are securely backed up to Amazon S3. Dropbox is kind of like taking the best elements of subversion, trac and rsync and making them "just work" for the average individual or team. Hackers have access to these tools, but normal people don't.

Zero buzzwords, named reference points, explicit user value, identifies who the user is.

**Basedash S20:**
> Basedash is a collaborative database interface that lets team members view and edit data directly from their production database. It's like Airtable but for your existing database.

2 sentences. X-but-Y does all the work.

### Location
"Where do you live now, and where would the company be based after YC?" Format: City A, Country A / City B, Country B. Plus explanation of the location decision.

YC accepts founders from anywhere. The batch requires San Francisco for 3 months.

## Progress

### "How far along are you?"
Current stage: idea, prototype, live product, revenue, profitable? State what exists now. Be specific.

**Dropbox:**
> Prototype done in Feb. Beta in people's hands now. Version I can charge for: 6-8 weeks.

**Basedash:**
> MVP fully developed, launched on Product Hunt 2 months ago.

### "How long have each of you been working on this? How much of that has been full-time?"
Per-founder, specific. "3 months part time. About ~5KLOC client and ~2KLOC server" (Dropbox). Quantify "part-time" - hours/week, or project output.

### "What tech stack are you using, or planning to use? Include AI models and AI coding tools you use." ⭐ NEW Summer 2026
YC explicitly wants to know about AI tooling. List the stack: languages, frameworks, infrastructure, AI APIs/models, AI coding tools (Claude Code, Cursor, Copilot). Don't sanitize this - if AI is central to how you build, say so. YC considers AI-native development a positive signal for Summer 2026.

### "Optional: attach a coding agent session you're particularly proud of." ⭐ EXPERIMENTAL Summer 2026
YC accepts .md or .txt exports from coding agents (Claude Code, Cursor, etc.). Use `/export` in Claude Code or equivalent in Cursor. Submit a session showing non-trivial AI-assisted engineering - problem diagnosis, architecture decisions, complex debugging. Max 25MB.

This is optional but a real differentiator. Most founders won't submit one. A strong session export shows how you think and how you work.

### "Are people using your product?" Yes/No

If Yes, reveals: **"How many active users or customers do you have? How many are paying? Who is paying you the most, and how much do they pay you?"** - single free-text field.

Rubric: numbers only. "300 signups, 14 paying, largest customer pays $500/mo" beats "growing user base with strong engagement." If none, answer should be No.

### "Do you have revenue?" Yes/No

If Yes, reveals three fields:

**6-month revenue table** - auto-labeled with the 6 most recent calendar months (e.g. Oct 2025 - Mar 2026), one USD$ input per month. Not cumulative. Not GMV. Your revenue. Enter 0 for zero-revenue months. Fill oldest first.

**"Where does your revenue come from?"** - breakdown by source if multiple (e.g. product A vs. product B, consulting vs. product). Skip if single source.

**"Anything else regarding your revenue or growth rate?"** - context that changes how numbers should be read. Use for: just signed enterprise pilots closing next month, deliberately throttled signups to fix bugs, one-time grant inflating a month.

**The bar is honesty, not impressive numbers.** Basedash got in with $30/month. State the actual numbers.

### Reapplication question
"If you are applying with the same idea as a previous batch, did anything change? If you applied with a different idea, why did you pivot and what did you learn from the last idea?"

N/A if first application. If reapplying: see `reapplying.md`. This is an opportunity, not a chore.

### Accelerators/incubators
Disclose any past programs. Name, what you got, where you are now.

## Idea

### "Why did you pick this idea? Do you have domain expertise? How do you know people need what you're making?"
The founder-market-fit question. YC wants to know you didn't find this on TechCrunch.

**Priority order:**
1. Personal need: "We had this problem ourselves." Strongest.
2. Deep domain experience: "I worked in this industry for X years and saw Y."
3. Direct user research: "We talked to 50 X and they all said Y."
4. Market data: weakest.

**Basedash:**
> I discovered the need for this product after working on another startup. We found there were no good tools for managing data with teams. We really wanted to use Airtable's interface for our own database.

Personal need + previous-startup origin.

### "Who are your competitors? What do you understand about your business that they don't?"
Don't pretend you have no competitors. "No one is doing this" = either bad market or no research.

**Rubric:**
- Name 3-5 real competitors. Mix of incumbents and startups.
- For each, briefly say why you win or coexist.
- Including big names (Google, Microsoft) is fine if you have a real argument. Dropbox listed Google's GDrive openly.

**Dropbox:**
> Carbonite and Mozy do a good job with hassle-free backup, and a move into sync would make sense. Sharpcast announced Hummingbird but are taking an extraordinarily difficult approach involving NT kernel drivers. Google's coming out with GDrive at some point. Microsoft's Groove does sync but is very heavyweight.

6 competitors named, each with specific reason for why Dropbox wins.

### "How do or will you make money? How much could you make?"
Business model + rough market size. Keep it short. A bottoms-up calculation works. YC sweet spot: $1-10B. Smaller = is it worth the effort? Larger = are you being realistic?

**Basedash:**
> Selling a subscription to companies for complete access to the product. Potentially as much as Tableau (~$1.2B).

### Category dropdown
Pick the closest. Don't agonize.

### "If you had any other ideas you considered applying with, please list them."
Paul Graham: "Often when we fund people it's to do something they list here and not in the main application."

2-3 ideas, 1-2 sentences each. Show range. Don't list things you'd pursue halfheartedly.

## Equity

### "Have you formed ANY legal entity yet?"
Yes/No. YC will help you incorporate as Delaware C-Corp if needed. Either answer is fine.

If Yes, reveals:
- **"Please list all legal entities..."** - free text. E.g. "Delaware C Corp (incorporated Jan 2025), German GmbH (for EU operations)." List every entity including subsidiaries.
- **"Please describe the equity ownership in percentages among founders, employees and other stockholders."** - free text, not a table. E.g. "Alice Chen (CEO) 45%, Bob Park (CTO) 45%, option pool 10%." Include titles. A roughly equal co-founder split is the strongest signal. Heavy imbalance (80/20) raises questions - address why if uneven.

### "Have you taken any investment yet?"
Yes/No.

If Yes, reveals:
- **"List any investments received."** - name, amount, premoney valuation/cap, type (convertible note, SAFE, stock), date. One investment per line.
- **"How much raised total in USD?"** - number only.
- **"How much money in the bank now?"** - number only.
- **"How much do you spend per month?"** - number only. Monthly burn.
- **"How long is your runway?"** - e.g. "5 months", "18 months".

### "Are you currently fundraising?"
Yes/No. Answer honestly - YC uses this to understand where you are in the funding process, not to screen you.

## Curious

### "What convinced you to apply to Y Combinator? Did someone encourage you to apply? Have you been to any YC events?"
Honest. If a YC alum encouraged you, name them. If you went to Startup School or a YC event, mention it.

### "How did you hear about Y Combinator?"
Honest. One or two sentences.

## Batch Preference

Select: Summer 2026 (July-September, San Francisco) OR A batch after Summer 2026.

If choosing a future batch, explain briefly why in the application - e.g. you'll be done with school or closing a major deal.

## Format reminders for every answer

- First sentence = TL;DR. Partners may not read past it.
- Concrete beats general; specific beats abstract
- Numbers, names, dates wherever possible
- 1-3 sentences for most fields; rarely more than 5
- No marketing-speak: "leveraging", "synergy", "next-gen", "AI-powered" without concrete content, "revolutionizing", "transforming"
- Each answer stands alone; don't reference other answers
- Honest beats polished. Partners detect fluff instantly.
