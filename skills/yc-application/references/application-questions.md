# YC Application Questions - Full Reference

This is the current YC application as of the 2025-2026 cycles. Questions are stable across batches; YC occasionally adjusts wording but the structure has been consistent for 15+ years. Word/character limits are based on the actual form fields. For each question: what it's literally asking, what the partner is *really* trying to learn, the rubric for a strong answer, and a real example from a successful application.

## Section A: Company

### A1. Company name
Just the name. Don't agonize. Founders rename later.

### A2. Company URL (if any)
Live website link. If none, leave blank - better than a placeholder Squarespace.

### A3. Demo URL
A video, screen recording, or live link showing the product working. **Statistically, applications with a demo are much more likely to get an interview.** Even a 90-second Loom of you clicking through the product beats nothing. If the product needs a login, include credentials.

### A4. Describe what your company does in 50 characters or less ⭐ HIGH STAKES
This is the elevator pitch. Partners use it to decide whether to keep reading. Goal: any reader has a clear picture in their head after 4 seconds.

**Rubric:**
- Concrete, not abstract ("Collaborative database interface for teams" ✓ "AI platform for enterprises" ✗)
- No buzzwords. No "revolutionizing", "AI-native", "platform", "next-gen"
- "X but for Y" is excellent if X is universally known (Uber, Airbnb, eBay, Slack, Excel work; Wolfram, Datadog, Stripe-for-non-tech-people don't)
- Read your draft to someone outside your industry. If they don't get it, rewrite.

**Good examples (from real applications):**
- "Collaborative database interface for teams." (Basedash, S20 ✓ accepted)
- "AI that writes SEC filings in 10 minutes." (illustrative)
- "It's like Stripe, but for B2B invoices in Latin America." (illustrative)

**Bad examples:**
- "AI-powered platform for enterprises" (means nothing)
- "We are revolutionizing the $3tn trade finance market" (marketing-speak, no content)

### A5. What is your company going to make? Please describe your product and what it does or will do. ⭐⭐ HIGHEST STAKES
**This is the most important question on the application.** It's also the first question of the interview. The partner reads this with the 50-char pitch in mind and decides whether to engage.

**Rubric:**
- 2-4 sentences. First sentence is a TL;DR.
- Matter-of-fact, not promotional. Test: after reading, can the reader reproduce the idea in their head?
- Use "X but Y" structure if it fits. Don't be afraid of seeming derivative.
- Cover what it currently is (1-2 sentences), what it will become (1 sentence). Skip the vision/mission language.
- Describe what users do with it, not what your tech is.

**Dropbox S07 (got accepted):**
> Dropbox synchronizes files across your/your team's computers. It's much better than uploading or email, because it's automatic, integrated into Windows, and fits into the way you already work. There's also a web interface, and the files are securely backed up to Amazon S3. Dropbox is kind of like taking the best elements of subversion, trac and rsync and making them "just work" for the average individual or team. Hackers have access to these tools, but normal people don't.

Notice: zero buzzwords, zero "revolutionizing", uses well-known reference points (subversion, trac, rsync), names the actual user value ("just works"), explicitly identifies who the user is ("the average individual or team").

**Basedash S20 (got accepted):**
> Basedash is a collaborative database interface that lets team members view and edit data directly from their production database. It's like Airtable but for your existing database.

Notice: 2 sentences. The X-but-Y comparison does most of the work. Anyone who knows Airtable instantly understands.

### A6. Where do you live now, and where would the company be based after YC?
Format: City A, Country A / City B, Country B. YC accepts founders from anywhere. The batch is in San Francisco; you must be there for the 3 months. After that, you can go anywhere. If you'll stay in SF, say so. If you'll return, say so. Don't be evasive.

### A7. Please tell us about the time you most successfully hacked some (non-computer) system to your advantage. ⭐ THE WILDCARD
Paul Graham explicitly says this question is a wildcard that can rescue an otherwise weak application. YC is looking for people who beat systems, not people who follow them.

**Rubric:**
- Be specific and concrete. Names, dates, what you did, what you got out of it.
- Doesn't have to be glamorous. "I got a 4-year visa by..." or "I negotiated a free year of office space by..." works.
- Should be slightly cheeky / show resourcefulness. The classier the move, the worse - they want hustle.
- Don't repeat your startup as the answer.

**Real examples that worked:**
- Basedash S20: "I managed to continue working on my company as my capstone project for school until I graduate. I also recruited a technical student as a partner working full-time hours for free (don't worry, they signed an NDA & IP agreement)."
- Various: convincing a school to count a startup as course credit; getting a visa via an obscure category; negotiating into an event for free; cold-emailing your way into a job; reverse-engineering a system for legitimate advantage.

### A8. Please tell us in one or two sentences about the most impressive thing other than this startup that you have built or achieved. ⭐ HIGH STAKES
Paul Graham: **"To me this is the most important question on the application."**

**Rubric:**
- It's not the type of achievement that matters - it's the magnitude.
- DON'T list the startup itself. They already know.
- Pick the thing that was hardest, preferably hardest *intellectually*. Doesn't matter if it's resume-able.
- Specific facts beat adjectives. Numbers, names, what was at stake.

**Dropbox S07:**
> Drew - Programming since age 5; startups since age 14; 1600 on SAT; started profitable online SAT prep company in college (accoladeprep.com). For fun last summer reverse engineered the software on a number of poker sites and wrote a real-money playing poker bot (it was about break-even).

Notice: 5 specific things in 2 sentences. Each one is a real, hard, verifiable thing.

**Bad answer:**
> Jordan is an exceptionally dedicated person who gives 100% effort to every project he undertakes.

(Paul Graham uses this verbatim as the example of what NOT to do.)

### A9. Founder video URL (1 minute, unlisted YouTube)
See `video-and-demo.md`. This is critical - applications with videos are more likely to get interviews.

### A10. Who writes code, or does other technical work on your product? Was any of it done by a non-founder?
**The hidden test:** Is there a technical founder? If 100% of the technical work is being done by contractors or non-founders, that's a major red flag for YC. Michael Seibel: "The founding team can't build an MVP and relies on external contractors" is one of the top three turn-offs.

**Rubric:**
- Best: a founder writes all code.
- OK: a founder writes most code, with limited help (e.g. an open source library, a friend doing one component under NDA).
- Risky: contractors built the core. Address this directly - explain the plan to bring tech in-house, or why this works for your specific case.

### A11. How many founders are on the team?
Number. YC accepts solo founders but the rate is lower. If solo, address it directly elsewhere - explain why solo, what the plan is for hires.

## Section B: Founders

### B1. Founder details (per founder)
Name, age, education (school, year, degree, subject - mark unfinished in parens), email, personal/GitHub/LinkedIn URLs, prior employers and titles. Main contact first. Asterisk anyone not able to move to SF for the batch.

**Don't sandbag this.** If you went to a great school or worked at a notable company, list it - it's not bragging, it's signal. If you didn't, list whatever you did and let the achievements section do the heavy lifting.

### B2. Most impressive thing each founder has built/achieved
See A8. Repeat for each co-founder. One sentence each. Specific.

### B3. Interesting project two or more of you created together
Critical for multi-founder teams. YC wants to see you've already worked together on something. Could be a side project, an old startup, a hackathon, a research paper. Include URLs.

If you've never built anything together, that's a serious gap. Address it: "We met 6 months ago and have built X together since" - anything beats "we haven't."

### B4. How long have the founders known one another and how did you meet?
Long-term friendships are a positive signal (you've already survived disagreements). Recent introductions aren't disqualifying but the bar is higher on having shipped something together.

### B5. Have any of the founders not met in person?
If yes, this is a yellow flag. Address why and when you'll meet.

### B6. Will the founders work exclusively on this for the next year?
Required: yes for at least one founder. "No school, no other jobs."

If you're a student, this might mean dropping out or deferring. YC has funded many students who did this. Address it directly.

### B7. Other commitments during the batch?
Be honest. A 2-week vacation booked is fine. A second job isn't.

### B8. Future commitments (school, grad school, etc.)?
Honest answer. "Finishing my MIT degree in May, then full-time on this" is fine. "Considering a PhD if this doesn't work" is a red flag.

## Section C: Progress

### C1. How long have each of you been working on this? Full-time / part-time?
Be specific. "3 months part time. About ~5KLOC client and ~2KLOC server of python, C++, Cheetah templates, installer scripts, etc." (Dropbox S07) - note how Drew quantifies "part-time" with actual lines of code.

### C2. How far along are you?
Are you live? In beta? Building? When will you launch?

**The bar isn't "have a polished product."** YC funds idea-stage companies. The bar is "we have a clear picture of what's done, what's not, and when the next thing ships."

Examples:
- "MVP fully developed, launched on Product Hunt 2 months ago." (Basedash)
- "Prototype done in Feb. Beta in people's hands now. Version I can charge for: 6-8 weeks." (Dropbox)

### C3. Do you have users?
Yes/no. If yes, see C4.

### C4. How many active users / customers? How many paying? Who pays the most?
Quantitative. State numbers. If the numbers are small, state them anyway. Basedash got in with $30/month in revenue and ~300 signups. The rule is honesty + specificity, not impressive numbers.

If you have notable customers (recognizable companies, YC alumni companies trying it, big enterprise pilots), name them.

### C5. Revenue questions (last 6 months, monthly)
Six fields, one per month. USD. "0" if none. **Cumulative is wrong; this is per-month.** GMV is wrong; this is your revenue, not transaction volume.

If revenue is non-existent, that's fine - say zero. Don't fudge.

### C6. Anything else about revenue or growth rate?
Use this to add context that changes how the numbers should be read. Examples:
- "Just got a small grant; focusing on marketing for the next 3 months." (Basedash)
- "Two enterprise pilots signed this week, $40k ACV each, will close in 30 days."
- "We deliberately throttled signups to fix a major bug; reopening next week."

### C7. Are you applying with the same idea as a previous batch? Did you pivot?
If new applicant, write N/A. If reapplying, see `reapplying.md` - this question is critical and is an opportunity, not a chore.

### C8. Have you participated in any other accelerator/incubator?
Disclose. State which one, what you got out of it (money? customers?), and where you are now. Hiding it is worse than admitting it.

## Section D: Idea

### D1. Why did you pick this idea? Do you have domain expertise? How do you know people need it?
**This is the founder-market-fit question.** YC wants to know you didn't pick this idea by reading TechCrunch.

**Rubric (in priority order):**
1. **Personal need:** "We had this problem ourselves." Strongest possible answer.
2. **Deep domain experience:** "I worked in this industry for X years and saw Y."
3. **Direct user research:** "We talked to 50 X and they all said Y."
4. **Weakest:** "We saw market data suggesting…"

**Basedash:**
> I discovered the need for this product after working on another startup. We found there were no good tools for managing data with teams. We really wanted to use Airtable's interface for our own database.

Notice: personal need + specific previous-startup origin.

### D2. What's new about what you're making? What substitutes do people use because it doesn't exist?
The substitutes question is the underrated half. **Make the alternatives sound painful.** That's how you signal real demand.

**Dropbox:**
> Most small teams have a few basic needs: (1) team members need their important stuff in front of them wherever they are, (2) everyone needs to be working on the latest version of a given document, (3) and team data needs to be protected from disaster. There are sync tools (e.g. beinsync, Foldershare), there are backup tools (Carbonite, Mozy), and there are web uploading/publishing tools (box.net, etc.), but there's no good integrated solution.

Notice: lists the unmet needs, then names the existing partial solutions, then identifies the gap.

### D3. Who are your competitors? Who do you fear most?
**Don't pretend you have no competitors.** Saying "no one is doing this" is a kiss of death - either it's a bad market or you haven't done your research.

**Rubric:**
- Name 3-5 real competitors. Mix of incumbents and startups.
- Pick the one you actually fear and say so.
- For each, briefly say why you can win or coexist.
- Including big names (Google, Microsoft) is fine if you have a real reason for why you'll win - Dropbox listed Google's GDrive openly.

**Dropbox:**
> Carbonite and Mozy do a good job with hassle-free backup, and a move into sync would make sense. Sharpcast (venture funded) announced a similar app called Hummingbird, but according to Jeff (who is good friends with the tech lead) they're taking an extraordinarily difficult approach involving NT kernel drivers. Google's coming out with GDrive at some point. Microsoft's Groove does sync and is part of Office 2007, but is very heavyweight and doesn't include any of the web stuff or backup. There are apps like Omnidrive and Titanize but the implementations are buggy or have bad UIs.

Notice: 6 competitors named, each with specific reason for why Dropbox wins or differentiates.

### D4. What do you understand about your business that others don't?
The "unique insight" question. Best answer: a non-obvious thing you believe that's a consequence of your specific experience, that competitors don't share.

**Dropbox:**
> Competing products work at the wrong layer of abstraction and/or force the user to constantly think and do things. The "online disk drive" abstraction sucks, because you can't work offline and the OS support is extremely brittle. Anything that depends on manual emailing/uploading (i.e. anything web-based) is a non-starter, because it's basically doing version control in your head.

**Basedash:**
> Software is built by teams, not individuals.

Both work. Dropbox's is technical. Basedash's is one sentence. Either is fine if it's a real insight.

### D5. How do or will you make money? How much could you make?
Rubric:
- State the business model. Common ones: SaaS subscription, transactional, marketplace take rate, usage-based, enterprise license, advertising.
- Estimate the upper bound with a bottoms-up calculation. "There are X potential customers. We can charge $Y/year. Market = $X*Y."
- A market size estimate of $1-10B is in the YC sweet spot. Smaller and they wonder if it's worth the time. Larger and they wonder if you're being realistic.

**Basedash:**
> Selling a subscription to companies for complete access to the product. Potentially as much as Tableau (~$1.2B).

One sentence each. Concrete reference point.

### D6. How will you get users? Chicken-and-egg problems?
For non-marketplace businesses, this is "what's your distribution plan." For marketplaces/networks, this is "how do you bootstrap liquidity."

If you have early traction with a specific channel, lead with it. If you have a marketplace, you need a real answer for the cold-start problem (do single-sided, paid acquisition for one side first, geographic concentration, etc.).

### D7. Which category best applies?
Dropdown. Pick the closest. Don't agonize.

## Section E: Equity

### E1. Have you formed a legal entity?
Yes/no. YC will help you re-incorporate as Delaware C-Corp later if needed. Either answer is fine.

### E2. Legal entities and where formed
List each. State or country.

### E3. Equity breakdown (founders, employees, other stockholders)
Percentages. Founder titles (CEO, CTO, etc.).

**A roughly equal split between cofounders is the strongest signal.** Heavy imbalance (e.g. 80/20) raises questions. If your split is uneven, address why.

### E4. Have you taken any investment? How much?
Disclose. Investor names, amounts, valuation/cap if SAFE.

### E5. How much do you spend per month? How much in the bank? Runway?
Numbers. Months of runway.

## Section F: Others

### F1. Other ideas you considered
Paul Graham: "Often when we fund people it's to do something they list here and not in the main application."

**Rubric:**
- 2-3 ideas, 1-2 sentences each.
- Show range - different domains is fine.
- Don't list things you'd half-heartedly pursue. Each should be something you'd be excited to work on.

### F2. Something surprising or amusing you've discovered (need not relate to the project)
Personality test. Be human.

**Examples that worked:**
- "The ridiculous things people name their documents to do versioning, like 'proposal v2 good revised NEW 11-15-06.doc', continue to crack me up." (Dropbox)
- "My last name is Musing which is slightly amusing." (Basedash)

Both are short. Both are personal. Neither is a humble-brag. Neither tries too hard.

### F3. What convinced you to apply to YC? Did anyone encourage you? Have you been to YC events?
Honest. If a YC alum encouraged you, name them - it helps. If not, say so. If you went to Startup School, mention it.

### F4. Referral code (if any)
A YC alum referral can help. It's not cheating; it's a positive signal. If you don't have one, leave blank - there's no penalty.

## Format reminders for every answer

- First sentence = TL;DR
- Concrete > general; specific > abstract
- Numbers, names, dates wherever possible
- 1-3 sentences for most fields; rarely more than 5
- No marketing-speak. No "leveraging", "synergy", "next-gen", "AI-powered" without concrete content, "revolutionizing", "transforming"
- Each answer stands alone; don't reference other answers
- Honest > polished. Partners detect fluff instantly.
