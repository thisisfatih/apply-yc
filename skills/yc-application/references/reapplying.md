# Reapplying to YC

Roughly 30% of accepted YC founders applied at least once before getting in. Reapplying is normal and not a disadvantage if handled well. The "what changed since last time" question is the single biggest opportunity in a re-application.

## Reapplying is not a disadvantage

Founders who got in after multiple tries:
- Drew Houston (Dropbox) - first-time application but referenced in many guides as the gold standard
- Many partners themselves applied multiple times before getting in with prior companies
- Various recent batches: Fluently founder applied 3 times before acceptance
- Basedash's Max Musing applied 4 times
- ~30% of S24 batch had applied before

YC explicitly says: "It's very common for teams to take our feedback, re-apply the following batch and get accepted."

## The two reapply scenarios

### Scenario A: Same idea, more progress
If the company is the same and has shipped, grown, or learned things since the last application: lead with the progress.

The "Are you applying with the same idea as before, or has anything changed?" question is your best slot. Use it to demonstrate momentum.

**Rubric:**
- 2-4 sentences.
- Quantitative. State new numbers vs. old: "When we applied in June, we had 0 paying customers. Now we have 14 paying customers and $4k MRR."
- Mention specific things you shipped.
- Mention any feedback from the previous YC rejection that you addressed.

**Strong example pattern:**
> Same idea, but significantly different company. When we applied in [batch], we had [previous state]. Since then we have [shipped X, hit Y revenue, signed Z customers]. We took your feedback that [specific thing] and have [specific response]. We're now [current state].

### Scenario B: Pivoted / new idea
If the company pivoted, this question is asking *why*, and what you learned.

**Rubric:**
- 2-4 sentences.
- Spend most of the words on the *learnings*, not the new idea (the new idea has its own questions).
- Be honest about why the previous one didn't work. "We couldn't get to $1k MRR after 8 months and concluded the buyer was too dispersed" is better than "we decided to pursue a bigger opportunity."
- Make the new idea feel like a logical consequence of what you learned, not a random new start.

**Strong example pattern:**
> We applied last [batch] with [previous company doing X]. We launched, got [specific data], and learned [specific insight]. That insight led us to [new company]. The connection: [the through-line in your domain expertise or user base].

## Using prior YC feedback

If you got an interview last time and were rejected, YC sent you written feedback. Read it carefully. Two failure modes to avoid:

1. **Overweighting the feedback as a checklist.** They might have flagged "no technical co-founder" - that's a real issue, but solving only that and reapplying without other progress isn't enough. The feedback is signal, not specification.

2. **Ignoring the feedback.** If they said "your market doesn't seem big enough" and you reapply with the same TAM analysis, that's a wasted reapplication.

**The right response:** address the specific feedback explicitly in the "what changed" answer. "You told us last batch that X was a concern. We've responded by Y, and here's the data on Z."

## Things that don't help in a reapplication

- Saying "we got really close last time, please give us another shot" - partners don't care about previous proximity, only current state
- A bigger TAM than before with no underlying change in the business
- A glossier version of the same application - they wanted progress, not better writing
- Listing many founders you've added if those founders aren't really in. Be careful adding co-founders you don't fully trust just to address a "no technical co-founder" feedback.

## When to reapply vs. wait

**Reapply this batch (deadline within 1-2 months) if:**
- You've shipped meaningful product progress
- You've added customers or users
- You've added a co-founder who fills a real gap
- You've pivoted in a way that responds to specific feedback

**Wait a batch or two if:**
- Nothing material has changed since last application
- You're in the middle of a big change (new co-founder onboarding, major pivot still in flux)
- You'd be reapplying with worse data than last time

## Detecting implicit reapplication signals

Founders don't always disclose prior YC contact in the reapplication field. Before critiquing any draft, scan the whole application for these signals:

**Explicit signals:**
- "Are you applying with the same idea as a previous batch?" field is filled in (not N/A)
- Prior accelerator section mentions a YC fellowship, YC interview, or YC rejection

**Implicit signals:**
- References to YC partners by first name in casual context ("Adora", "Dalton", "Harj") — implies direct contact
- Amusing discovery or wildcard answer references a prior YC interview experience
- Application shows unusually strong structural awareness of what YC looks for — suggests prior feedback loop
- "What convinced you to apply?" answer references a previous application or rejection specifically

**What to do when you detect implicit signals:**

1. Check whether the "what changed since last time" question is answered. If it's blank or says N/A but implicit signals exist, flag it: the founder is leaving their biggest opportunity unanswered.

2. Reframe the critique accordingly. A reapplication is evaluated on *delta* — what changed since last time — not just on current state. A flat "12 paying customers" answer reads differently if last batch they had zero and YC told them traction was the gap.

3. If you detect signals but the founder hasn't told you explicitly, ask one direct question before critiquing: "Have you applied to YC before? If so, what feedback did you get?" Don't guess — the answer changes how to read every section.

4. If prior YC interview is confirmed: the "impressive thing" and "interesting project together" answers may have already been evaluated. Their weak points were likely noted. Flag anything that looks unchanged from a standard first-application structure.

## When to apply with a new entity

If your previous company genuinely failed and you're starting something new:
- It's still a reapplication if you're a returning founder. Don't pretend it's your first time.
- Use the "I've applied before with X" question to disclose. Hiding it is worse than disclosing it.
- The "impressive thing" question is now a great opportunity - your previous startup, even if it failed, is impressive if you shipped something real and learned from it.
