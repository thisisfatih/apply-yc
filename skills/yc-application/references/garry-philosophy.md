# Garry Tan's Operating Philosophy (applied to YC applications)

Garry Tan is the current CEO of YC. He publishes his thinking at garryslist.org and his operating system for building companies is the foundation of gStack (garrytan/gstack on GitHub). These principles show up directly in how he evaluates founders. Load this file when coaching a founder on vision, team framing, or AI-native positioning.

---

## Boil the Ocean

The old advice "don't try to boil the ocean" was for a world where doing the complete thing was expensive. AI inverts that.

Garry's thesis (garryslist.org, Feb 2026):

> "If your plan is to keep doing what you're doing, AI is terrifying. If your plan is to build something dramatically bigger, it's the best news you've ever gotten."

**For YC applications:** Don't half-answer. Don't write one version of the 50-char pitch and assume the partner fills in the blanks. When AI makes the marginal cost of doing a complete, excellent application near-zero, incomplete is a choice to be smaller. Produce 3-5 versions of every high-stakes answer. Research competitors by name. Write the "impressive thing" five times and pick one.

The principle from gStack (Garry's own tool):

> "Always do the complete thing when AI makes the marginal cost near-zero."

---

## Jevons Paradox at the company level

Efficiency gains don't reduce consumption - they expand it. Steam engines made coal cheaper; coal consumption exploded. AI makes software cheaper; software demand will explode.

**For YC applications:** Don't describe doing the same thing cheaper. Describe what becomes possible when the efficiency gain gets passed to scale. A company that replaces one analyst with AI isn't a 10x company. A company that replaces every analyst in a sector, then handles the resulting explosion in demand, is.

When the founder describes their vision (D4, D5), ask: does it name the Jevons effect? "We're replacing manual X with AI" is table stakes. "We're replacing manual X with AI, which creates 10x more demand for X, which we're already positioned to handle" is a YC-legible vision.

## Ephemeralization

Buckminster Fuller, 1938: "more with less through technology." Each innovation wave lets a smaller team build what required a larger one. Garry applies this directly to the current AI moment.

Historical sequence: stone arches → steel cables. Steam → electricity. Assembly line → software. Software factory (spec + tests + AI agents generating implementation) → now.

**For YC applications:** A 2-person team shipping what used to take 20 is an ephemeralization story. State the ratio explicitly. "Two engineers. Shipped [product] in 6 weeks. [N] paying customers. Here's why this is possible now and wasn't two years ago."

This framing belongs in the team section and the "why now" framing of D2.

## Builder voice, not consultant voice

The most visible operational principle in how Garry communicates - and exactly what YC partners are trained to filter for.

From gStack (Garry's operating principles):

> "Sound like a builder, not a consultant. Name the file, the function, the command. No filler, no throat-clearing. Short paragraphs. End with what to do."

In applications, this maps directly:

| Consultant | Builder |
|-----------|---------|
| "driving significant user engagement" | "12 paying customers, $400 MRR" |
| "we believe there's a massive opportunity" | "there are 50,000 companies with this problem; we're charging $500/mo" |
| "leveraging AI to transform X" | "2 engineers, shipped X in 6 weeks, using Claude Code" |
| "our mission is to empower..." | "we make [specific thing] for [specific user]" |
| "we're excited to" | [cut it] |

Red-pen test: count the verbs "transform", "leverage", "enable", "empower", "revolutionize", "disrupt". Each one is a deduction. Replace each with a number or a fact.

## "Obvious to insiders" heuristic

From Garry's public commentary: if you work intensively with AI, it's obvious the tools are dramatically further along than outside observers believe. Founders applying to YC should communicate from this vantage point - not as missionaries explaining AI's potential, but as practitioners who already live in the future.

The best YC applications don't try to convince partners AI is powerful. They show what they've already done with it. "Shipped X in 6 weeks, 2 engineers, Claude Code" is more persuasive than any framing about AI potential.

This is why the Summer 2026 form added an optional coding agent session export. YC is explicitly rewarding founders who can demonstrate they already live here.

## Application checklist (Garry's lens)

Run this after the full critique pass, as a final filter.

**Completeness check.** Before critiquing quality: is every question answered? Video uploaded? All founder bios filled? An incomplete application is the fastest rejection. When AI makes completion near-free, incomplete = a choice to be smaller.

**10x framing check.** Does the vision answer describe something only possible with AI, at a scale not possible before? "We do X but better" fails. "X at 1000x the scale of the current leader, possible because AI replaced the 200 analysts they need" passes.

**Jevons check.** Does the answer name what new demand gets unlocked by the efficiency gain? Not required but strong if present.

**Ephemeralization ratio.** Can you state team size vs. output in the team section? "2 people, $1M ARR, automated X" is a signal. "8 engineers and growing fast" is noise.

**Builder voice audit.** Read every answer aloud. Does it sound like something the founder would say to a YC partner over lunch, or like a pitch deck? Any sentence that sounds like a pitch deck: rewrite it as a lunch conversation.

**Completeness of each answer.** Each answer should be as complete as AI makes possible. Not bloated - complete. The question is: if a partner asked a follow-up on any sentence, is the answer already there? If not, add it. The marginal cost of adding one more sentence is near-zero.
