# YC Interview Preparation

If the founder got an interview, the goal of this section is to **calibrate them away from over-preparation**. YC explicitly says over-prepared interviews go worse, and that the highest-leverage prep is making real product/customer progress between the application and the interview.

## The format

- **10 minutes.** Not 30. Not 15. Ten.
- **Zoom call.** All founders on camera.
- **2-3 YC partners.** They've read your application and have it open.
- **No presentations, no slide decks.** They explicitly don't want them.
- **Conversation-style.** They ask questions, you answer. They may ask to see a demo.

## What YC actually says about preparation

From YC's official Interview Guide and from Dalton Caldwell's video (which they explicitly recommend):

**Don't rehearse.** "Founders looking for an edge sometimes think that doing lots of interview preparation will help. However, beyond the basic preparation recommended here, it is not useful and is often counter-productive." Over-prepared founders answer questions before they're fully asked. They sound rehearsed. The interviewers are trying to identify teams they want to work closely with for years - naturalness matters.

**Instead of rehearsing, make progress.** The single best thing you can do between the application and the interview is have something new to report. Launched. Got a customer. Hit a revenue milestone. Shipped a feature. "Move fast and make quick progress is the most surefire way to impress your interviewers."

**Be ready to describe what you do.** First question is almost always "What is your company working on?" - yes, the same question they read the answer to in your application. They want to hear you say it.

## What partners typically ask

Compiled from Michael Seibel's advice and partner interviews:

### Opening (always)
- "What is your company working on?"
- (For multi-founder teams) "How did you meet?" or "How long have you known each other?"

### Product / users (if you've launched)
- "Where do new users come from?"
- "What is your growth like?" (week-over-week, month-over-month)
- "How much are users using the product? Are they sticking around?"
- "What are your unit economics?"
- "What makes new users try you?"
- "Why do reluctant ones hold back?"
- "What are the top things users want?"
- "What has surprised you about user behavior?"

### Idea / market
- "Who are your competitors?"
- "Why will customers switch from [incumbent]?"
- "Why now? What changed?"
- "What happens if Google/Microsoft/Stripe builds this?"
- "What's the biggest risk to this business?"

### Founders
- "Why you for this idea?"
- "Why is this person your co-founder?"
- "What's the role split?"

### Hard ones
- "If we don't fund you, what will you do?" - correct answer: "Build the company anyway. We're not contingent on YC."
- "Walk me through your numbers." - they want it cold. ARR, MRR, growth rate, churn, CAC, runway.

## Hard rules for the interview

**Know your numbers cold.** ARR, MRR, churn rate, CAC if you have it, number of customers, average ACV, burn rate, runway. Have them written down somewhere you can glance at. YC explicitly allows this - they say "having them written down will free you from feeling like you have to memorize every little number."

**One founder per question minimum (for multi-founder teams).** YC prefers each founder answer at least one question. If your CEO is dominating, the partners will steer questions to other founders. Don't fight it.

**Be honest about challenges.** "We often ask about obstacles and we tend to be more convinced by candid discussion of difficulties than glib dismissal of them." Trying to hand-wave a real risk is worse than naming it.

**Disagree respectfully.** If a partner says something incorrect about your business, push back politely. They respect founders with conviction. Folding under pressure is a bad signal.

**Have the demo loaded and ready to share-screen.** Don't fumble for it. If hardware, have it on the desk. If they don't ask, that's fine - but be ready.

**Brevity.** Answer in 1-3 sentences when possible. Long answers will be interrupted. Treat each question as if you have 20 seconds of airtime before they cut you off.

## Things that hurt interviews

- Rehearsed-sounding answers, especially if they don't quite match the question asked
- Speaking over the partners
- One founder dominating while others stay silent
- Inflated metrics (numbers you can't verify) - partners may follow up post-interview to verify
- "We have no competitors"
- Overlong answers that don't get to the point
- Pretending not to see an obvious risk
- Fumbling the demo
- "We were just discussing this, let me check with my co-founder" (decisions during the interview signal the team isn't aligned)

## What to do the night before

- Re-read your application. They have it open; you should remember exactly what you wrote.
- Put your key numbers on one piece of paper next to your laptop.
- Have your demo loaded in a tab and tested.
- Sleep. The interview is short and high-stakes; over-tired founders ramble.
- DO NOT do mock interviews extensively. One run-through is fine. Eight is too many.

## What to do in the 24-48 hours after

YC sends decisions same-day or within a day or two. Either way:

- **If accepted:** YC has a fast onboarding process. Be ready to move quickly on incorporation paperwork, banking, and the SAFE.
- **If rejected:** YC sends written feedback. **Read it carefully.** Many YC founders re-applied after a rejection and got in. The feedback is genuinely meant to be useful, not pro-forma. Garry Tan and partners have repeatedly said "many companies that got in had been rejected before."

## A note on confidence calibration

Interview is a coin flip even for strong applications. Roughly 25-40% of interviewees get offers. So:
- Going in: don't pre-celebrate. Stay focused on the questions.
- Coming out: don't read tea leaves. Founders consistently misjudge how interviews went. Resist the urge to interpret a partner's tone or specific question.
- After: keep working on the company regardless of outcome. The single best thing you can have done is to have launched / shipped / closed a customer in the days after - that's evidence you'd get into a future batch even if this one doesn't work out.
